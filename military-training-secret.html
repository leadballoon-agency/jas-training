The Military Training Method That Builds Real Confidence

Why a Reservist Regimental Sergeant Major with 36 years of service trains civilians differently than everyone else

Joe Sims has a problem with most first aid training.

After 36 years as a Reservist Regimental Sergeant Major in the British Army, he's watched thousands of soldiers learn life-saving skills under the most demanding conditions imaginable.

Then he started looking at civilian first aid courses.

"I was shocked," Joe admits. "People would sit in a classroom for a day, practice on a dummy a few times, get their certificate, and somehow that was supposed to prepare them for a real emergency."

"Anyone can remember the steps when they're calm and relaxed. But emergencies aren't calm and relaxed. Your heart is racing, people are panicking, and you've got seconds to make decisions that could save someone's life."

The problem isn't the information being taught. The problem is how it's taught.

The Military Difference

In military training, there's a fundamental principle that most civilian instructors completely ignore: competence under pressure.

This is why military medical training looks nothing like civilian first aid courses.

1. Repetition Until It's Automatic

In the military, you don't practice a technique once or twice. You practice it until you can do it perfectly while exhausted, stressed, and under pressure.

"We'd make soldiers practice bandaging wounds in the dark, in the rain, while people were shouting at them," Joe recalls. "Because that's closer to what a real emergency actually feels like."

Most civilian courses? You practice chest compressions on a dummy for maybe 10 minutes, then move on to the next topic.

2. Realistic Scenario Training

Military medics train with scenarios that are deliberately chaotic and stressful. Fake blood, loud noises, multiple casualties, time pressure - everything designed to simulate the confusion of a real emergency.

"If you can save a life when everything's going wrong around you, you can definitely save one when conditions are better," Joe explains.

Compare that to civilian training where scenarios are neat, predictable, and usually involve just one calm casualty lying on the floor.

3. Confidence Through Competence

The goal of military training isn't just to teach knowledge - it's to build unshakeable confidence.

"When a soldier knows they can handle the worst-case scenario, they don't freeze up in lesser emergencies," says Joe. "They act immediately and effectively because they've been there before, even if only in training."

What This Means for Welsh Businesses

Joe started applying military training principles to his civilian courses after seeing too many "trained" first aiders panic in real situations.

"I watched a factory supervisor with a valid first aid certificate completely freeze when an employee cut their hand badly. They knew what they should do, but they couldn't make themselves do it."

The difference in his approach is obvious from the first minute:

Joe's Military Training Includes:

  • More Hands-On Practice: 70% practical vs 20% in standard courses
  • Realistic Pressure: Time limits, distractions, multiple casualties
  • Confidence Building: Mental resilience training alongside practical skills
  • Real Experience: Learn from someone who's used these skills in combat zones

The Results Speak for Themselves

"I can spot someone who's been through military-style training immediately," notes Sarah Williams, an NHS paramedic who's worked with Joe's trainees. "They're calm, focused, and they actually know what they're doing."

The difference becomes obvious during renewals.

Students who completed traditional courses often admit they've forgotten most of what they learned and wouldn't feel confident using their skills.

Joe's trainees? They remember everything and say they'd act without hesitation.

Why Most Instructors Don't Train This Way

It's harder.

Setting up realistic scenarios takes more time and effort than classroom lectures.

Building confidence requires understanding how people react under stress - something most civilian instructors have never experienced themselves.

"I've been in real emergencies," Joe explains. "I know what it feels like when your training either works or fails. Most instructors are teaching theory they've never had to use."

The Military Standard Test

Joe has a simple test for any first aid training:

"If you wouldn't trust your own family member's life to someone with this training, then the training isn't good enough."

Would you trust a family member's life to someone who:

  • Practiced chest compressions for 10 minutes six months ago?
  • Has never dealt with a panicking casualty?
  • Learned everything in a calm classroom environment?
  • Got their knowledge from videos and demonstrations?

Or would you prefer someone who:

  • Practiced until the techniques became automatic?
  • Trained under realistic pressure and stress?
  • Built genuine confidence through repetition?
  • Learned from someone with real emergency experience?

What You Can Do Right Now

If your business has first aid trained staff, here's a simple reality check:

Ask your first aiders honestly: "If someone had a heart attack right now, would you feel completely confident performing CPR?"

If the answer is anything other than "Absolutely, yes" - then your training wasn't enough.

Real emergencies don't wait for you to feel ready.
